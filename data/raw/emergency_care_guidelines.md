# Emergency Care Guidelines

## Overview
These guidelines help patients understand when to seek emergency care and what to expect in the emergency department.

## When to Seek Emergency Care

### Call 911 or Go to Emergency Department Immediately For:

**Life-Threatening Symptoms**
- Unconsciousness or unresponsiveness
- Difficulty breathing or shortness of breath at rest
- Chest pain or pressure
- Severe allergic reaction (swelling, rash, difficulty breathing)
- Sudden severe headache (worst headache of life)
- Numbness, weakness, or slurred speech (signs of stroke)
- Loss of vision or eye pain
- Severe abdominal or back pain
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Poisoning or overdose

**Trauma or Injury**
- Severe head, neck, or spinal injury
- Severe burns
- Severe fractures
- Crush injuries
- Multiple injuries from accident

**Psychiatric Emergencies**
- Suicidal thoughts or behavior
- Homicidal thoughts or threats
- Severe mental status change
- Dangerous behavior toward self or others

**Medical Emergencies**
- Severe diabetic symptoms (unresponsiveness, severe hypoglycemia)
- Severe dehydration
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat with symptoms
- Stroke symptoms (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time)
- Severe infection or high fever with confusion
- Placental abnormalities in pregnancy
- Preterm labor or severe pregnancy complications

### Call 911 If:
- Unsure whether situation is emergency
- Symptoms suddenly worsen
- Patient cannot walk to car safely
- Patient may deteriorate during transport
- Condition life-threatening
- Response time critical to outcome

### Go to Emergency Department If:
- Severe pain not managed at home
- Injury that may need imaging or specialist evaluation
- Severe allergic reaction
- Possible broken bones or severe sprains
- Deep wounds requiring stitches
- Acute illness not managed at urgent care

### Urgent Care Instead of Emergency Department:
- Mild to moderate injuries
- Cold or flu-like illness
- Urinary tract infection symptoms
- Strep throat symptoms
- Minor burns
- Sprains or muscle strain
- Rashes or skin infections
- Medication side effects
- Can wait 30-60 minutes without worsening

## Stroke Recognition - FAST

**F - Face Drooping**
- Smile and check if face is uneven
- Drop on one side indicates possible stroke

**A - Arm Weakness**
- Raise both arms
- Does one arm drift downward?
- Weakness in one arm indicates possible stroke

**S - Speech Difficulty**
- Repeat simple phrase
- Is speech slurred?
- Inability to speak or understand indicates possible stroke

**T - Time to Call 911**
- Note time symptoms started
- Call 911 immediately
- Time critical for stroke treatment (3-4.5 hour window)

## Heart Attack Recognition

### Chest Discomfort
- Pressure, squeezing, or heaviness in chest
- May last more than a few minutes
- May come and go

### Other Symptoms
- Shortness of breath
- Nausea or lightheadedness
- Cold sweats
- Discomfort in arms, jaw, neck, or back
- More common in men; women may have different symptoms

### Immediate Actions
- Chew aspirin if not allergic (unless told not to)
- Call 911 immediately
- Do not drive yourself
- Sit or lie down
- Try to remain calm

## Arriving at Emergency Department

### Check-In Process
1. Provide ID and insurance information
2. Describe chief complaint
3. May be asked to provide emergency contact
4. Placed in waiting area if not critical
5. Triaged to determine urgency

### Triage Assessment
- Vital signs taken (blood pressure, pulse, temperature, breathing)
- Brief history obtained
- Assigned acuity level (most urgent to least urgent)
- Severity determines order of evaluation

### Waits in Emergency Department
- All patients triaged immediately
- Most urgent cases seen first
- Less urgent cases may wait hours
- Waiting does not mean problem not serious
- Reassess if condition worsens

## During Emergency Evaluation

### What to Expect
- Vital signs monitored continuously
- Physical examination by provider
- Blood tests and urinalysis usually obtained
- EKG or imaging may be done
- IV may be started for fluids or medications
- Pain management provided

### Provider Discussion
- History and symptoms reviewed
- Allergies confirmed
- Current medications discussed
- Recent surgeries or hospitalizations noted
- Family history relevant to presentation

### Testing and Diagnosis
- Tests performed to determine cause
- Results reviewed by provider
- Diagnosis made
- Treatment plan determined
- Patient informed of results and plan

## Payment and Insurance

### Before Leaving Emergency Department
- Ask about charges and estimated costs
- Clarify what insurance covers
- Ask if prior authorization needed
- Understand any copay or coinsurance
- Request itemized bill if desired

### After Emergency Visit
- Bill mailed within 30-45 days
- Review for errors
- Contact billing with questions
- Payment plan available if needed
- Financial assistance available for uninsured/underinsured

## Common Emergency Department Conditions

### Chest Pain
- EKG and troponin to rule out heart attack
- Chest X-ray to check for pneumonia or collapsed lung
- Blood tests as needed
- Observation may be required

### Shortness of Breath
- Oxygen saturation checked
- Chest X-ray to check lungs
- EKG to check heart
- Blood tests as needed
- Oxygen provided if needed

### Severe Headache
- Blood pressure checked (may indicate hypertensive emergency)
- Possible CT scan to rule out bleeding
- Lumbar puncture if meningitis suspected
- Medications for pain and nausea

### Abdominal Pain
- Abdominal examination performed
- Ultrasound or CT scan may be ordered
- Blood tests to check for infection or other abnormalities
- IV fluids for dehydration
- Pain medications provided

### Trauma/Injury
- Immediate assessment for life threats
- Spinal precautions if applicable
- Imaging (X-rays, CT scans) as needed
- Wound care and stitches if needed
- Pain management provided

### Severe Allergic Reaction
- Epinephrine administered immediately
- IV started for fluids and medications
- Antihistamines given
- Steroids administered
- Observation for delayed reaction

## Emergency Department Do's and Don'ts

### Do's
- Tell provider about all symptoms
- Mention all medications and supplements
- Inform about allergies
- Tell provider if on blood thinners
- Inform about drug or alcohol use
- Ask questions if unclear
- Bring insurance card and ID
- Have emergency contact information available

### Don'ts
- Don't wait for pain to become severe
- Don't hide information from providers
- Don't demand specific tests or treatments
- Don't leave without being cleared (AMA - Against Medical Advice)
- Don't refuse recommended treatments
- Don't use emergency department for non-urgent care if possible

## Discharge Instructions

### What You'll Receive
- Written discharge instructions
- Medication prescriptions
- Follow-up appointment information
- Return precautions (when to return)
- Activity restrictions

### Follow-Up Care
- Schedule appointment with primary care provider within 1-2 weeks
- Keep follow-up appointments scheduled
- Fill prescriptions as directed
- Take medications as prescribed
- Monitor symptoms as instructed

### When to Return to Emergency Department
- Return for any of original emergency symptoms
- New severe symptoms develop
- Medications cause severe reactions
- Discharge instructions not clear

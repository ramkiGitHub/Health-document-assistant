# Patient Discharge Guidelines

## Document Status

- Document type: Synthetic healthcare guideline
- Intended use: Training and demo use only
- Contains real patient data: No
- Effective date: January 1, 2026
- Department: Care Coordination

## Purpose

This guideline describes the standard discharge planning process for patients leaving a hospital, observation unit, or outpatient procedure area. The goal is to support safe transitions of care and reduce avoidable readmissions.

## Scope

This guideline applies to discharge planning, patient education, medication review, follow-up scheduling, transportation planning, and communication with caregivers.

## Discharge Readiness

Before discharge, the care team should confirm that the patient is clinically ready, discharge orders are complete, follow-up needs are identified, and the patient or caregiver understands the discharge plan.

Discharge should not proceed if critical instructions are missing, urgent symptoms are unresolved, or the patient requires additional clinical review.

## Discharge Instructions

Written discharge instructions should include diagnosis or visit reason, medications, activity guidance, diet guidance, wound care if applicable, follow-up appointments, warning signs, and contact information for questions.

Instructions should be written in clear language and reviewed with the patient or caregiver before leaving the facility.

## Medication Review

The care team should review the discharge medication list with the patient or caregiver. The review should identify new medications, changed medications, stopped medications, continuing medications, allergies, and refill needs.

Patients should be advised to ask a licensed clinician before changing medication instructions.

## Follow-Up Appointments

The discharge plan should include follow-up appointments when needed. The appointment details should include provider name, clinic location, date, time, and preparation instructions.

If the appointment is not scheduled before discharge, the patient should receive instructions for scheduling and a recommended timeframe.

## Warning Signs

Discharge instructions should list warning signs that require urgent attention. Examples may include chest pain, severe shortness of breath, uncontrolled bleeding, fainting, severe allergic reaction, confusion, high fever, or worsening symptoms.

Emergency warning signs should direct the patient to emergency services rather than routine scheduling.

## Caregiver Involvement

If the patient needs assistance after discharge, the care team should include an authorized caregiver in discharge education when possible. Caregiver involvement should follow patient consent and privacy requirements.

## Transportation And Home Needs

The discharge team should confirm transportation arrangements and identify home needs such as mobility support, wound supplies, oxygen equipment, home health services, or durable medical equipment.

Unresolved home safety concerns should be escalated to care coordination before discharge.

## Documentation

The discharge record should document discharge diagnosis, instructions provided, medication review, patient understanding, follow-up plan, caregiver involvement, and any unresolved concerns.

## Escalation

Discharge concerns involving unsafe home environment, medication confusion, missing follow-up, inability to understand instructions, or urgent symptoms should be escalated to the provider or care coordination team.

## Safety Notice

This synthetic guideline is for learning and demonstration only. It is not medical advice and should not be used for real patient discharge decisions.

# Emergency Care Guidance Policy

## Document Status

- Document type: Synthetic healthcare guidance
- Intended use: Training and demo use only
- Contains real patient data: No
- Effective date: January 1, 2026
- Department: Emergency Services

## Purpose

This policy describes general routing guidance for urgent and emergency symptoms. The goal is to help staff direct patients to the appropriate level of care and avoid delays in emergency response.

## Scope

This policy applies to administrative staff, call center teams, clinic front desks, and digital assistant workflows. It does not authorize non-clinical staff or AI systems to diagnose, triage, or provide treatment advice.

## Emergency Symptoms

Patients reporting emergency symptoms should be directed to emergency services immediately. Emergency symptoms may include chest pain, severe shortness of breath, signs of stroke, severe bleeding, loss of consciousness, severe allergic reaction, major injury, seizure, severe confusion, or suicidal intent.

Staff should not schedule routine appointments for emergency symptoms.

## Stroke Warning Signs

Possible stroke warning signs may include face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty, sudden confusion, sudden vision changes, sudden severe headache, or sudden trouble walking.

If stroke symptoms are reported, staff should direct the patient to call emergency services immediately.

## Chest Pain Or Breathing Difficulty

Patients reporting chest pain, pressure, severe shortness of breath, blue lips, fainting, or severe weakness should be directed to emergency services immediately.

Administrative staff should not attempt to determine whether the symptoms are serious.

## Severe Allergic Reaction

Symptoms such as swelling of the face or throat, trouble breathing, widespread hives, severe dizziness, or collapse may indicate a severe allergic reaction. Patients reporting these symptoms should be directed to emergency services immediately.

## Mental Health Emergency

Patients reporting immediate danger to themselves or others, suicidal intent, or inability to stay safe should be directed to emergency services or a crisis line according to local procedure.

Staff should remain calm, keep the person engaged when possible, and escalate to a supervisor or clinician immediately.

## Non-Emergency Urgent Concerns

Some concerns may need same-day clinical review but may not be emergencies. Examples include worsening symptoms, fever after a procedure, medication reaction without breathing difficulty, or new pain after discharge.

Non-clinical staff should route these concerns to the nurse line, on-call clinician, or urgent care workflow according to local policy.

## AI Assistant Behavior

An AI assistant must not diagnose, assign severity, or provide treatment instructions. If emergency symptoms are mentioned, the assistant should advise the user to contact emergency services immediately and should not continue routine document Q&A.

If the question is unclear but may involve urgent symptoms, the assistant should escalate to a human clinician or emergency workflow.

## Documentation

Staff should document the reported concern, time of contact, instructions provided, escalation path, and supervisor or clinician notified when applicable.

## Escalation

Emergency symptoms must be escalated immediately. Unclear urgent symptoms, clinical questions, safety concerns, or mental health crisis statements should be escalated to a clinician, supervisor, crisis workflow, or emergency services according to policy.

## Safety Notice

This synthetic policy is for learning and demonstration only. It is not medical advice, triage advice, or emergency care instruction.

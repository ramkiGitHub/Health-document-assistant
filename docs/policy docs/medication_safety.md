# Medication Safety And Review Policy

## Document Status

- Document type: Synthetic healthcare policy
- Intended use: Training and demo use only
- Contains real patient data: No
- Effective date: January 1, 2026
- Department: Pharmacy Services

## Purpose

This policy describes medication safety practices intended to reduce medication errors, improve documentation, and support safe communication between patients, providers, and pharmacy teams.

## Scope

This policy applies to medication review, medication reconciliation, prescription renewals, allergy documentation, high-alert medication handling, and patient education in outpatient and inpatient settings.

## Medication Reconciliation

Medication reconciliation should be performed at registration, admission, transfer, discharge, and follow-up visits when applicable. The care team should compare the patient's current medication list with newly ordered medications and documented allergies.

The medication list should include prescription medications, over-the-counter medications, supplements, herbal products, dose, route, frequency, and last known use when available.

## Allergy And Adverse Reaction Documentation

Known allergies and adverse medication reactions must be documented clearly in the patient's record. Documentation should include the medication or substance, reaction type, severity, date reported, and source of information.

If allergy information is unavailable, the record should indicate that allergy status could not be confirmed. The care team should not assume that missing allergy information means no known allergies.

## High-Alert Medications

High-alert medications require additional verification because errors may cause serious harm. Examples may include anticoagulants, insulin, opioids, concentrated electrolytes, and chemotherapy agents.

High-alert medication orders should be reviewed for patient identity, medication name, dose, route, frequency, indication, allergies, interactions, and duplicate therapy.

## Prescription Renewal Requests

Prescription renewal requests should include patient name, date of birth, medication name, current dose, pharmacy information, and reason for renewal. The provider may require recent lab results, visit history, or clinical review before approving a renewal.

Urgent renewal requests should be routed according to clinic workflow. Patients should be advised not to change or stop medication unless instructed by a licensed clinician.

## Patient Education

Patients should receive medication instructions in clear language. Education may include medication purpose, dose, timing, common side effects, storage instructions, refill process, and when to contact the clinic.

Patients should be encouraged to ask questions and report side effects, missed doses, duplicate medications, or confusion about instructions.

## Medication Changes

Medication changes must be documented with the new medication name, dose, route, frequency, start date, stop date when applicable, and reason for change. Discontinued medications should be clearly marked to reduce confusion.

The assistant or administrative staff must not recommend medication changes. Questions about starting, stopping, or changing medication must be escalated to a licensed clinician.

## Look-Alike And Sound-Alike Medications

Medication names that look or sound similar should be handled carefully. Staff should use tall-man lettering or additional verification steps when required by local policy.

## Escalation

Medication questions involving side effects, allergic reactions, missed doses, pregnancy, surgery, medication changes, duplicate therapy, or urgent symptoms should be escalated to a licensed clinician or emergency services as appropriate.

## Safety Notice

This synthetic policy is for learning and demonstration only. It is not medical advice. Medication decisions must be made by qualified healthcare professionals.
